Percentage Formulas Explained: Master All Types of Percentage Calculations

By FreebieCalculator Team August 20, 2025 8 min read

Percentages are everywhere in daily life - from calculating tips and discounts to understanding interest rates and test scores. This comprehensive guide breaks down all the essential percentage formulas with clear examples and practical applications.

Basic Percentage Formula

The fundamental percentage formula forms the foundation for all percentage calculations:

Percentage = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100

This formula tells us what percentage one number is of another. For example, if you scored 85 out of 100 on a test:

Example 1: Test Score

Percentage = (85 ÷ 100) × 100 = 85%

Finding the Percentage of a Number

When you need to calculate a specific percentage of a number, use this formula:

Result = (Percentage ÷ 100) × Number

Example 2: Sales Tax

What is 8.5% of $150?

Result = (8.5 ÷ 100) × 150 = 0.085 × 150 = $12.75

Percentage Increase Formula

To calculate how much something has increased as a percentage:

Percentage Increase = ((New Value - Original Value) ÷ Original Value) × 100

Example 3: Salary Increase

Your salary increased from $50,000 to $55,000:

Percentage Increase = ((55,000 - 50,000) ÷ 50,000) × 100

= (5,000 ÷ 50,000) × 100 = 10%

Percentage Decrease Formula

Similar to increase, but for calculating reductions:

Percentage Decrease = ((Original Value - New Value) ÷ Original Value) × 100

Example 4: Discount Calculation

A jacket's price dropped from $120 to $90:

Percentage Decrease = ((120 - 90) ÷ 120) × 100

= (30 ÷ 120) × 100 = 25%

Percentage Change Formula

For general percentage change (positive or negative):

Percentage Change = ((New Value - Original Value) ÷ Original Value) × 100

This formula gives positive results for increases and negative results for decreases.

Finding the Original Value

When you know the final value and percentage change, you can find the original:

Original Value = Final Value ÷ (1 + Percentage Change/100)

Example 5: Reverse Calculation

After a 20% increase, the price is $60. What was the original price?

Original Value = 60 ÷ (1 + 20/100) = 60 ÷ 1.2 = $50

Compound Percentage Calculations

For multiple percentage changes applied in sequence:

Final Value = Original × (1 + Rate₁/100) × (1 + Rate₂/100) × ...

Example 6: Multiple Changes

A stock price increases 10%, then decreases 5%:

Starting at $100: 100 × 1.10 × 0.95 = $104.50

Common Percentage Applications

Tips and Gratuity

Calculate tips using: Tip = Bill Amount × (Tip Percentage ÷ 100)

Interest Calculations

Simple interest: Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Grade Calculations

Grade Percentage = (Points Earned ÷ Total Points) × 100

Business Metrics

Profit margin, growth rates, and efficiency ratios all use percentage formulas.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing percentage points with percentages: A change from 10% to 15% is a 5 percentage point increase, but a 50% relative increase.
  • Wrong base for calculations: Always identify which value is your reference point (original vs. new).
  • Decimal errors: Remember to divide percentages by 100 when using them in calculations.
  • Rounding too early: Keep full precision until the final result.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can percentages be greater than 100%?

Yes! Percentages over 100% indicate that the part is larger than the original whole. For example, a 150% increase means the new value is 2.5 times the original.

What's the difference between percentage and percentage points?

Percentage points measure absolute changes in percentages, while percentages measure relative changes. If unemployment rises from 5% to 6%, that's a 1 percentage point increase or a 20% relative increase.

How do I calculate backwards from a percentage?

To find the original value from a final value and percentage change, divide the final value by (1 + percentage change/100).
